The Challenge

From Data Fatigue to Performance Clarity

Most fitness apps focus on the workout. SeleraFit focuses on the workflow. I identified three core friction points in the current endurance ecosystem:

  • Manual Syncing: Athletes shouldn't have to be data entry clerks.
  • Generic Progressions: "One-size-fits-all" plans lead to injury and burnout, especially in the 40+ demographic.
  • The Feedback Gap: The lag between a completed workout and a coach's review.


Core Feature Deep Dives

A. High-Density Data Visualization

The UX Problem: Standard heart rate and pace graphs are difficult to interpret while "in the red" (during high-intensity training).

The Solution: I designed a Multi-Signal Glanceable UI. By using color-coded intensity zones and simplified "Readiness Scores," athletes can make split-second decisions without breaking their stride.

The Logic of Color & Cognition

In endurance training, cognitive load increases as physical intensity rises. I developed a high-contrast, color-coded UI system where Green = Overall Fitness Level, Orange = Fatigue, and Blue = Form. This allows athletes to process critical performance data in under 200ms while operating at peak heart rates.





B. AI-Augmented Contribution Flows

The UX Problem: Adding new workouts to a plan is traditionally a high-friction, multi-click process.

The Solution: Using an AI-native interface, SeleraFit allows coaches to "speak" a workout into existence. The system automatically parses the intensity, duration, and physiological goal, mapping it instantly to the athlete's calendar.

C. The Longevity Logic (Focus on 40+)

The UX Problem: Most apps ignore the specific recovery needs of aging athletes.

The Solution: Integrated Auto-regulatory Training logic. The UI dynamically adjusts workout difficulty based on the athlete's self-reported "Vibe" and recovery metrics, prioritizing longevity over short-term PR's.

Built a "Vibe Check" feedback loop that uses subjective RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) to override planned intensities. If an athlete reports low recovery, the UI dynamically suggests a "De-load" alternative, preventing over-training syndrome—a critical UX requirement for the 40+ demographic.
